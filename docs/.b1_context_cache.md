## RISE JARS PRODUCT SUMMARY

### What Rise Jars Is
- **Product:** Line of premium borosilicate glass food storage jars engineered as a modular, stackable system
- **Materials:** Borosilicate glass (thermal expansion 3× lower than soda-lime; oven/freezer/fridge safe, no leaching, no staining, dishwasher-safe indefinitely). Airtight food-grade silicone gasket + locking lid system. Single-hand operable seal.
- **System design:** Jars in 3–5 coordinated sizes share lid-to-base geometry enabling stable vertical stacking and efficient grouping by diameter. Engineered modular architecture — not a design flourish, an engineering constraint.
- **Target market:** Primary: health-conscious women 25–45, meal preppers who treat kitchen as identity expression, shop Whole Foods/farmers markets, follow clean-eating content, already own premium kitchen tools. Secondary: sustainability-focused buyers (30–50) replacing plastic permanently. Adjacent: gift buyers ($60–$150 premium kitchen gifts).
- **Key differentiators:** (1) Borosilicate vs. soda-lime (Pyrex US reformulated in 1998 to cheaper glass — unknown to most consumers); (2) First glass storage system designed from ground up for modularity — no competitor does this; (3) Lifestyle/premium positioning ("last food storage system you'll ever buy") vs. utility positioning of incumbents; (4) Authentic sustainability story (permanent replacement for plastic).
- **Long-term vision:** Full premium modular kitchen organization brand (jars → canisters → produce storage → pantry → fridge drawer systems). Distribution: DTC first, specialty retail (Williams-Sonoma, Sur La Table, Whole Foods), Amazon, licensing of modular system architecture.
- **Current dev status at Part A time:** Concept complete, no prototype built, no manufacturing partner, patent research not initiated, brand name "Rise Jars" chosen, business entity not formed, bootstrapped.

### Key Facts from Part A: What Atlas Validated in Stages 1–4

**Stage 1 — Idea Capture**  
- Atlas must capture three distinct innovation layers: material (borosilicate), system design (modular stacking), positioning (premium lifestyle). A brief that collapses this to "glass jars with tight lids" fails.
- Key caveat: "They stack" is not a mechanism description. Stacking geometry must be specified conceptually before Stage 1 is complete.
- Idea Brief confirmed; Initial Competitive Snapshot (Pyrex, OXO, Weck) created; Recommended Focus Areas: (1) will target customer pay premium? (2) is problem acute enough to switch? (3) who are real competitors and what's displacement cost?
- Atlas strengths: structured problem capture separates three innovation layers. Gap: software-centric mechanism description framework; no physical product spec module; no lifestyle/positioning capture.

**Stage 2 — Validation**  
- Core risk: validation theater. Health-conscious consumer will say "yes" to almost any premium glass concept. Price point must be tested directly (not "would you buy this?" but "here at $129, what would need to be true?"). Requires at least 3 interviews with skeptics.
- Five assumptions tested: premium price supportability (highest risk), problem acuity, borosilicate differentiation relevance to customer, modular design as primary vs. nice-to-have, target market reachability.
- Atlas strength: explicit validation theater guardrail. Gap: no structured price sensitivity testing (Van Westendorp or Gabor-Granger); no channel validation prompt; no physical prototype test protocol.

**Stage 3 — Market Research**  
- Core challenge: "food storage" TAM is tens of billions and irrelevant. Must size the premium, health-conscious, glass segment specifically — no standard industry report covers this. Must build from components (US meal prep population × annual spend × premium capture rate).
- SAM must segment DTC year 1 vs. retail years 2–3 (retail requires MOQ, slotting fees, net-90 terms).
- Key risk: price compression from Amazon private label / Asian DTC within 12–18 months of launch success. Competitive moat must be brand, not just product.
- Atlas gap: no CPG-specific market sizing methodology; no retail channel economics module; no competitive positioning map output.

**Stage 4 — Patent Readiness**  
- IP landscape: (1) Utility patent on modular stacking mechanism — heavily patented space, novelty unconfirmed; (2) Design patent on visual form — more practical/cost-effective for early stage consumer goods; (3) Trademark: "Rise" is common in food/health brands, clearance required in Class 21 (kitchen utensils); (4) Trade dress long-term.
- Critical risk: manufacturing NDA required before sharing designs. Any public disclosure before filing starts a 1-year US clock.
- Atlas gap: design patent guidance thin; no NDA generator; no IP landscape visualization; no trademark monitoring integration.

**Overall findings — Atlas Stages 1–4:**  
- Strengths: structured problem capture, validation theater detection, prior art self-research framework, three-state readiness evaluation, explicit handoff documentation.
- Weaknesses: software-centric specification framework, no price testing structure, market sizing assumes findable category data, thin design patent coverage, no NDA generation.
- Missing (ranked Critical): physical product specification workflow, price sensitivity testing workflow, physical product readiness report, price sensitivity summary report, physical product specification data fields, willingness-to-pay data structure, physical innovation disclosure document, price architecture document.

## ATLAS ARCHITECTURE SUMMARY

### Engine Owns Progress Principle
The backend is the source of truth. Atlas tracks readiness, flags gaps, and drives the journey forward. The inventor focuses on the invention — not on remembering where they are or what comes next. The engine never lets the inventor get lost. One clear next action, always.

### Definition of Done — 6 Gates (from Roadmap)
Each stage completion requires:
1. All stage-specific completion criteria checked off
2. Primary deliverable document finalized and confirmed by inventor
3. Readiness evaluation reaches "Ready to Move Forward" state
4. Handoff package prepared (named documents passed to next stage)
5. Atlas Assessment committed to invention record
6. Inventor has made a documented decision (go/no-go, IP strategy, etc.)

*(Note: The roadmap and blueprints describe criteria-based completion, not a numbered 6-gate list by that name — this summary reflects the consistent pattern across all stages.)*

### UX Principles (from Roadmap)
- **Calm, premium, minimal:** Atlas is a mentor-collaborator, not a form builder. The experience must feel like working with a trusted expert, not filling out a government application.
- **One CTA per screen:** The inventor always knows exactly what to do next. One clear next action at all times.
- **Structured options, not open prompts:** Atlas presents choices with trade-offs made explicit rather than asking open-ended questions that overwhelm. "Should this prioritize durability or portability? You can't do both at this price point."
- **Conversation before forms:** Natural dialogue is preferred; structured fields are generated automatically from conversation where possible.
- **Reduce cognitive load:** Atlas asks at every step: can this disappear? Can Atlas complete this automatically? Can it be simplified?
- **Generate before requesting:** Atlas attempts to generate documents before asking the inventor to create them.

### Stage Config Pattern (enabled flag)
All 15 stages are defined in the system. Stages 1–4 are live. Stages 5–15 are queued and will be enabled one complete stage at a time. Each stage is independently valuable. The journey is always the destination — incremental delivery is a release strategy, not a compromise. Stage unlock is controlled by an `enabled` flag per stage in the backend config.

### Non-Negotiables
- Backend is source of truth — never the UI or the founder's memory
- Every stage produces tangible, usable deliverables (not homework, not instructions)
- Every stage leaves the inventor better prepared than before
- Atlas never asks twice for information it has already collected
- Documents accumulate into a living invention record that grows richer with every stage
- Atlas always answers: "What should I do next?"

## STAGE 05 — PRODUCT DESIGN

### Purpose and Primary Objective
**Purpose:** Transform the validated concept into a defined product specification — form, function, materials, and user experience — ready for prototyping.  
**Primary objective:** Produce a Product Design Specification detailed enough for a prototyper, industrial designer, or engineering firm to begin work without needing to ask foundational questions.

### Success Definition
Stage is successful when the Product Design Specification is complete enough to hand to a skilled prototyper and that prototyper can begin work without coming back with questions about what the product does, who it's for, or what materials to use.

### Completion Criteria
- [ ] Functional specification complete: all core product functions defined with success criteria
- [ ] Physical specification defines form factor, materials, dimensions, and weight target
- [ ] User experience specification covers primary user interaction from first encounter through completed use
- [ ] At least one visual reference (sketch, reference photo, or wireframe) attached
- [ ] Design Decision Log complete for all major decisions
- [ ] Open Design Questions list finalized and each item categorized: resolve before prototyping or resolve during
- [ ] No critical design decision remains undocumented

### Key Data Fields / Inputs the Stage Collects
- Idea Brief from Stage 1 (core mechanism and function)
- Customer Segment Profiles from Stage 3 (use context, expectations)
- Patent Readiness Report from Stage 4 (novel elements to protect, prior art design constraints)
- Inventor's sketches, reference images, or inspiration products
- Inventor-stated constraints: target retail price, materials preferences, size requirements, regulatory environment (FDA, CE, FCC, etc.)
- Manufacturing context: preferred geography, access to specific manufacturing processes

### AI Capabilities Defined in the Blueprint
- **Functional specification structuring** — translating invention concept into defined functions with success criteria
- **Physical specification guidance** — prompting decisions on form factor, materials, dimensions, weight
- **User experience specification** — mapping primary user journey from first encounter through ongoing use
- **Manufacturing implication analysis** — real-time consequence surfacing for design decisions (e.g., "ABS injection molding implies $3,000–$8,000 tooling and 1,000-unit MOQ")
- **Design Decision Log generation** — recording every major decision with alternatives considered and rationale
- **Design completeness evaluation** — identifying gaps against a complete specification standard
- **Open Design Questions list generation** — prioritized: must resolve before prototyping vs. during
- **Specialist Engagement Flag identification** — recognizing when professional expertise is required (materials engineering, electrical design, regulatory compliance, food-contact certification)

### Documents/Reports the Stage Produces
- **Product Design Specification** — master design document covering function, form, materials, UX; single source of truth for all prototype and manufacturing work
- **Design Decision Log** — audit trail of design reasoning; every major choice, alternatives considered, rationale
- **Open Design Questions** — prioritized handoff checklist for the prototyper; becomes the prototype's testing agenda
- **Specialist Engagement Recommendations** — specific professional roles or firms to engage based on design complexity

### Handoff to Stage 6
Product Design Specification, Design Decision Log, Open Design Questions (testing agenda), visual references, Specialist Engagement Recommendations, materials/manufacturing decisions (for Stage 7 supplier identification).

### Readiness Evaluation States
- **Not Ready:** Functional spec incomplete, no physical spec, no UX spec started, no visual reference, multiple critical decisions undocumented
- **Getting There:** Mostly complete functional spec lacking some success criteria, partial physical spec, primary UX interaction covered but secondary undefined, at least one visual reference, Decision Log started
- **Ready to Move Forward:** All criteria above met

### Future Integrations Planned
CAD tools (Onshape, Fusion 360 API), open-source component search (Thingiverse, GrabCAD), materials database (MatWeb), industrial design firm marketplace, AI visual mockup generation.

## STAGE 06 — PROTOTYPE

### Purpose and Primary Objective
**Purpose:** Build and test a physical or digital representation of the product to validate the design before committing to manufacturing tooling and investment.  
**Primary objective:** Produce a tested prototype with documented learnings that either confirms the Product Design Specification or produces a revised specification incorporating what was discovered.

### Success Definition
Stage is successful when at least one prototype has been built and tested, all Open Design Questions from Stage 5 have been addressed, and the inventor has made a documented decision — based on prototype evidence, not assumption — to proceed to manufacturing.

### Completion Criteria
- [ ] At least one physical or digital prototype built
- [ ] All Open Design Questions from Stage 5 addressed — answered by prototype or formally deferred with documented rationale
- [ ] Prototype test results documented with pass/fail outcomes for each test protocol
- [ ] Any design changes required by prototype findings documented and incorporated into the Product Design Specification
- [ ] Prototype Readiness Assessment complete
- [ ] Inventor has made a documented decision to proceed to manufacturing

### Key Data Fields / Inputs the Stage Collects
- Product Design Specification from Stage 5 (complete design document to build from)
- Open Design Questions from Stage 5 (specific questions the prototype must answer)
- Inventor's prototyping resources: available skills, equipment, fabrication access, budget, timeline
- Any previous prototype attempts and their documented shortcomings
- Specialist inputs if Stage 5 flagged specialist engagement requirements

### AI Capabilities Defined in the Blueprint
- **Prototype type recommendation** — based on Open Design Questions and available resources; chooses among: concept prototype (communicates idea), functional prototype (tests mechanism), looks-like prototype (tests form/aesthetics/ergonomics), works-like prototype (tests core engineering close to final form)
- **Prototype test protocol design** — defining measurable pass/fail criteria for each Open Design Question; prevents "build and then decide if it seems fine"
- **Prototyping method recommendations** — per component: 3D printing, laser cutting, CNC, off-the-shelf, electronic breadboard, etc.
- **Prototype findings documentation guidance** — structured questions that draw out specific, actionable observations (what worked as predicted, what was unexpected, what must change)
- **Prototype-to-Spec Gap Analysis** — comparing what the design predicted vs. what the prototype revealed
- **Design revision guidance** — helping inventor update the Product Design Specification based on prototype findings; produces versioned revision
- **Prototype Readiness Assessment** — evaluating whether prototype results support proceeding to manufacturing

### Documents/Reports the Stage Produces
- **Prototype Plan** — testing agenda committed before prototyping begins; what to build, what questions to answer, pass/fail criteria for each; establishes the standard against which results are evaluated
- **Prototype Test Report** — documented findings from all prototype testing; pass/fail outcomes for each test; proof-of-concept record
- **Prototype-to-Spec Gap Analysis** — documented delta between what the design specified and what the prototype revealed
- **Revised Product Design Specification** — if applicable; versioned against Stage 5 original with all design changes documented
- **Prototype Readiness Assessment** — Atlas's judgment on whether results support proceeding to manufacturing

### Handoff to Stage 7
Finalized (post-prototype) Product Design Specification (the version manufacturers quote against — must be post-prototype, not pre-prototype draft), Prototype Test Report (what the product must do in real use; manufacturers need this for performance requirements), Revised Product Design Specification if applicable, materials/component sourcing research from prototyping, known design complexity flags (aspects that proved difficult to prototype foreshadow manufacturing challenges).

### Readiness Evaluation States
- **Not Ready:** No prototype built, no testing against Open Design Questions, findings not documented, design change decision not addressed
- **Getting There:** At least one prototype built, some questions tested but not all, findings partially documented, design changes noted but not incorporated
- **Ready to Move Forward:** All criteria above met — prototype built and tested, all questions addressed, results documented with pass/fail, design changes incorporated, documented decision to proceed

### Future Integrations Planned
3D printing service APIs (Hubs, Xometry, Shapeways), PCB prototyping integration (PCBWay, OSH Park), prototype video and photo documentation, AI prototype analysis (photo upload → root cause + design remediation), user testing panel (target-audience users submit structured feedback through Atlas).

## STAGE 07 — MANUFACTURING

### Purpose and Primary Objective
**Purpose:** Identify and qualify a manufacturing partner, establish production economics, and create a viable, documented path from prototype to manufactured product.  
**Primary objective:** Select a qualified manufacturing partner, establish unit economics across at least two production volumes, and produce a documented manufacturing plan ready to execute.

### Success Definition
Stage is successful when the inventor has received at least two manufacturer quotes against a complete RFQ, selected a manufacturing partner with documented rationale, and produced a Unit Economics Model that makes the business math clear at initial and scale production volumes.

### Completion Criteria
- [ ] RFQ document complete and sent to at least 3 manufacturers
- [ ] At least 2 manufacturer quotes received and evaluated against the scorecard
- [ ] Unit Economics Model complete for at least two production volumes
- [ ] Manufacturing partner selected with documented rationale
- [ ] Manufacturing agreement terms reviewed against Atlas's checklist
- [ ] Initial production order quantity defined and consistent with sales forecast
- [ ] Manufacturing Readiness Assessment complete

### Key Data Fields / Inputs the Stage Collects
- Finalized Product Design Specification from Stage 6 (prototype-validated; what manufacturers quote against)
- Prototype Test Report from Stage 6 (performance requirements)
- Materials and components list from design specification
- Target unit cost (inventor's estimate or early Stage 10 pricing work)
- Target production volumes: initial run and scale
- Geographic preferences: domestic, overseas, or both
- Any existing manufacturer relationships

### AI Capabilities Defined in the Blueprint
- **RFQ document generation** — from Product Design Specification; translates inventor's design into manufacturer-facing format including technical specs, materials, tolerances, testing/quality standards, target quantities, delivery requirements
- **Manufacturer type recommendation** — matching product category and scale to appropriate manufacturing model (domestic vs. overseas, contract manufacturer vs. OEM vs. ODM); trade-offs across cost, quality control, lead time, IP risk, MOQ
- **Unit economics modeling** — cost-per-unit at multiple production volumes (500, 2,000, 10,000 units); component-level breakdown: materials, labor, overhead, tooling amortization, logistics
- **Manufacturer quote evaluation and comparison** — structured evaluation: unit cost at requested volumes, tooling cost, payment terms, lead time, MOQ, quality certifications, communication responsiveness
- **Manufacturer Evaluation Scorecard generation** — consistent framework for comparing manufacturer bids
- **Manufacturing agreement terms checklist and gap analysis** — IP ownership/assignment, quality standards and defect remediation, delivery terms and penalties, payment terms, exclusivity provisions, NDA coverage
- **Break-even production volume calculation**
- **Manufacturing Readiness Assessment**

### Documents/Reports the Stage Produces
- **Request for Quote (RFQ) Document** — manufacturer-ready technical and commercial document; professional and complete; signals competence to manufacturer and earns serious quotes
- **Unit Economics Model** — cost-per-unit estimates at multiple production volumes with component-level breakdown; carries forward to Stage 10 (Pricing) as primary cost input and Stage 13 (Funding) as financial model foundation
- **Manufacturer Evaluation Scorecard** — structured framework for comparing manufacturer quotes on consistent basis
- **Manufacturing Plan** — selected manufacturer, initial order quantity, production timeline, payment schedule
- **Manufacturing Readiness Assessment** — Atlas's evaluation of readiness to place a production order

### Handoff to Subsequent Stages
- → Stage 10 (Pricing): Unit Economics Model is primary cost input
- → Stage 14 (Launch): Manufacturing Plan for logistics planning and inventory readiness
- → Stage 9 (IP): Manufacturing agreement review findings for IP clause structuring
- → Stage 13 (Funding): Unit Economics Model as financial model foundation; initial production order quantity for use-of-proceeds
- Manufacturer contact record: live relationships carried throughout remaining stages

### Readiness Evaluation States
- **Not Ready:** RFQ not completed, no manufacturers contacted, no unit economics calculated, no partner selection in progress
- **Getting There:** RFQ complete and sent to at least one manufacturer, at least one quote received but second pending, unit economics partially complete, manufacturer under evaluation but no selection documented
- **Ready to Move Forward:** All criteria above met

### Future Integrations Planned
Domestic manufacturer databases (Thomasnet, Maker's Row), overseas supplier verification (Alibaba Business Verification API), instant quoting services (Xometry, Protolabs for CNC/injection molding/sheet metal), freight and logistics cost estimation (Flexport for international scenarios), manufacturing trade show calendar by product category and geography.

## PRODUCT ROADMAP NOTES

### Architecture and Principles (relevant to Stages 5–7)
- All 15 stages are defined and queued. Stages 5–7 are enabled one complete stage at a time.
- The roadmap states: "The architecture is complete." Blueprint-to-implementation is the work.
- Product principles that bear directly on Stages 5–7: one clear next action, reduce uncertainty, engine owns progress, backend is source of truth, every stage produces tangible value.

### Roadmap Items Relevant to Stages 5–7

**Stage 5 Future Integrations (from roadmap):**
- CAD tools integration (Onshape, Fusion 360 API) — attach CAD files to design specification, version alongside written spec
- Thingiverse / GrabCAD search — surface open-source component designs to accelerate prototyping
- Materials database (MatWeb) — query material properties for informed materials selection
- Industrial design firm marketplace — post specification, receive quotes from partner design firms
- AI rendering — generate visual mockups from design specification description before physical prototyping

**Stage 6 Future Integrations (from roadmap):**
- 3D printing services API (Hubs, Xometry, Shapeways) — request prototype quotes without leaving Atlas
- PCB prototyping integration (PCBWay, OSH Park) — for electronics, design and order prototype boards in-platform
- Prototype video upload — attach demonstration videos and photos to test report as permanent records
- Rapid-iteration AI analysis — upload prototype photos, receive AI root cause + design remediation options
- User testing panel — recruit target-audience users to test prototypes and submit structured feedback through Atlas

**Stage 7 Future Integrations (from roadmap):**
- Thomasnet / Maker's Row API — search domestic manufacturers by product category from within Atlas
- Alibaba supplier verification API — cross-reference overseas manufacturers against verified supplier data
- Xometry / Protolabs API — instant quoting for CNC, injection molding, sheet metal fabrication within Atlas
- Trade show and manufacturer event calendar — surface relevant expos by product category
- Freight forwarder integration (Flexport) — generate shipping cost estimates for international manufacturing

### Planned AI Capabilities for Stages 5–7 (synthesis)
The roadmap describes a progression toward AI doing more of the work at each stage. For Stages 5–7, the planned trajectory includes:

- **Design stage:** AI generates visual mockups from spec descriptions; AI queries materials databases automatically; AI flags manufacturing implications in real time without waiting for inventor to ask
- **Prototype stage:** AI analyzes prototype failure photos; AI recruits and manages a user testing panel; AI compares Prototype Test Report against Design Specification automatically and flags gaps
- **Manufacturing stage:** AI generates the RFQ automatically from the Product Design Specification; AI cross-references manufacturer quotes against a database of known manufacturer performance; AI calculates unit economics from component costs without founder input; AI identifies IP risk clauses in manufacturing agreements before the founder reviews them

### Key Dependencies Across Stages 5–7
- Stage 5 → Stage 6: Product Design Specification + Open Design Questions are the primary handoff. Nothing starts in Stage 6 without them.
- Stage 6 → Stage 7: Post-prototype validated Product Design Specification (not pre-prototype draft) + Prototype Test Report are the primary handoff. Manufacturer conversations should not begin with an unvalidated spec.
- Stage 7 → Stage 10: Unit Economics Model is the primary cost input to pricing. These stages have a documented dependency.
- Stage 7 → Stage 9: Manufacturing agreement IP provisions must be reviewed against IP strategy from Stage 4. This is a critical cross-stage dependency that Atlas must surface proactively.
