# Stage 8 — Branding

---

## 1. Stage Overview

### Purpose

Stage 8 defines the Rise Jars brand identity — everything that shapes how the product is perceived before, during, and after purchase. This is not a logo exercise. It is the construction of a coherent brand system: a name that survives trademark clearance, a voice that sounds like someone the customer trusts, a visual language that signals premium without being intimidating, and a positioning statement sharp enough to guide every marketing, packaging, and sales decision downstream.

Brand identity is established at this stage — before pricing, before marketing, before sales — because every downstream customer-facing decision depends on a clear answer to: *Who is Rise Jars, and why does it exist?*

### Primary Objective

Produce a complete **Brand Identity System** for Rise Jars: trademark-tested brand name confirmed, brand positioning statement finalized, brand voice guide built, and visual direction brief ready to hand to a designer or photographer — all forming a single coherent character.

### Success Definition

Success in Stage 8 means that anyone joining the Rise Jars team — a copywriter, a packaging designer, a retailer buyer, a social media manager — can read the Brand Identity System and immediately understand how to represent the brand correctly, without additional explanation. The brand character is so clearly articulated that execution becomes a matter of following direction, not guessing intent.

---

## 2. What Rise Jars Needs

Rise Jars enters Stage 8 with a significant head start compared to most inventors:

- **Name is already chosen.** "Rise Jars" was selected in the concept phase and has not changed. Stage 8's job is to confirm it holds up under full trademark scrutiny and to build the complete identity around it — not to start from scratch.
- **Customer segment clarity.** Stage 3 produced Customer Segment Profiles: health-conscious women 25–45 who treat their kitchen as identity expression, and sustainability-focused buyers who want to eliminate plastic permanently. These profiles are the anchor for every brand decision.
- **Product story is differentiated.** The combination of (1) borosilicate glass, (2) modular stackable architecture, and (3) premium lifestyle aesthetics has no current occupant in the market. Rise Jars' brand needs to own that intersection — not generically claim "health" or "sustainability," but claim the specific truth: this is the glass storage system designed for the way you actually cook and live.
- **Price tier is premium.** At $80–$150/set versus OXO/Pyrex at $25–$40, Rise Jars' brand must justify a 3–5× price premium. That premium is real — it comes from material quality, engineering, and design — but the brand must communicate that value clearly. Customers do not automatically understand why borosilicate glass costs more, or why the modular lid system is architecturally different from a drawer full of mismatched lids.
- **Visual language needs definition.** The product is physically beautiful — clear glass, clean geometry, stacking precision, premium hardware. The visual direction brief must translate that product reality into a photographic and typographic language that can be applied consistently across packaging, DTC site, social, and retail environments.

**Specific brand identity decisions Rise Jars must make in Stage 8:**
- Brand archetype selection (Caregiver, Sage, or a blend) — this determines every downstream voice and visual decision
- Positioning statement finalized — the sentence that contains the entire competitive strategy
- Voice attribute selection — the 3–4 qualities that distinguish Rise Jars' tone from every generic wellness brand
- Visual direction definition — palette registers, typographic mood, photography world, design sensibility

## 3. Questions Atlas Should Ask

Atlas should lead Rise Jars through these questions in a structured conversation. Atlas does NOT ask all of these at once — it groups them logically and generates its own answers where research is possible, surfacing only decisions that genuinely require the founder's judgment.

1. **When your target customer opens Instagram and sees a piece of content from Rise Jars — what do they feel in the first three seconds?** *(Captures the emotional register Atlas cannot infer from data alone.)*

2. **Who is Rise Jars more like — Hedley & Bennett (a kitchen brand that takes craft seriously), Great Jones (a kitchen brand that leads with joy and color), or Le Creuset (a kitchen brand that leads with heritage and permanence)?** *(Forces archetype calibration against real brand analogues.)*

3. **Is Rise Jars the brand that says "you deserve better" or "you already know better"?** *(Distinguishes aspiration-led voice from authority/sage voice — critical for tone.)*

4. **When you imagine the Rise Jars customer five years from now, are they still using the same jars they bought at launch — or have they expanded their collection?** *(Surfaces whether "system/ecosystem" is part of the brand story or just a product feature.)*

5. **What should Rise Jars never, ever say?** *(Anti-positioning — defines voice guardrails. What are the words, phrases, or tones that would make the brand feel cheap, generic, or wrong?)*

6. **How important is the borosilicate glass story in your marketing — is it a headline claim ("Made from real borosilicate glass") or a proof point behind a bigger brand idea?** *(Determines whether material safety leads the narrative or supports it.)*

7. **Does Rise Jars want to be recognized at the kitchen store shelf, the farmers market booth, and the Instagram grid with the same visual language — or are these different visual contexts?** *(Multi-channel visual consistency vs. contextual adaptation.)*

8. **Are there any brands — in ANY category, not just kitchen — whose visual language you want Rise Jars to feel adjacent to?** *(Opens the visual reference world beyond kitchen CPG — could surface luxury skincare, Scandinavian home goods, premium food brands, etc.)*

9. **What is the single most important thing a first-time Rise Jars customer should tell a friend about why they bought it?** *(Extracts the core value proposition as experienced by the customer, not described by the founder.)*

10. **At the moment someone sees the Rise Jars name for the first time — what should "Rise" mean to them?** *(Tests whether the name is doing the work intended — and whether it needs support from tagline or design to complete its meaning.)*

11. **If Rise Jars were a magazine, what magazine would it be?** *(Classic brand archetype triangulation tool — surfaces aesthetic sensibility and audience taste level.)*

12. **As the brand expands into canisters, pantry containers, and a full kitchen storage system — should the Rise Jars brand stretch to cover all of it, or does the long-term vision require a family brand architecture?** *(Seeds Stage 11+ brand architecture planning while the identity is still malleable.)*

## 11. Risks Discovered

### Risk 1 — Trademark Conflict for "Rise"
"Rise" is a common word used broadly in brand names across food, wellness, and kitchen categories. USPTO Class 21 may contain existing marks with "Rise" or "Rise [product category]" that create confusion risk. Stage 4 produced an initial clearance brief — Stage 8 requires a comprehensive search, not a preliminary one. If a blocking conflict exists, the brand name decision must be resolved before $80,000+ in marketing investment is made against the name.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Critical.*

### Risk 2 — Brand Archetype Misalignment with Premium Price
If Rise Jars adopts a brand archetype that does not support premium pricing — for example, leaning too far into the "Caregiver" archetype without any Sage authority, the brand risks feeling like a mid-market wellness brand rather than a premium kitchen system. At $80–$150/set, the brand must communicate that paying 3–5× the price of OXO glass is the right decision for someone who takes their kitchen seriously. A warm-but-not-authoritative brand character cannot carry that argument.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: High.*

### Risk 3 — Generic Wellness Visual Language
The health/wellness market is saturated with a specific visual language: soft beige, sans-serif typography, aspirational lifestyle photography with natural light. This language is so commonly used that it has become invisible. If Rise Jars' visual direction defaults to wellness-category conventions, it will fail to differentiate in DTC and social channels despite having a genuinely differentiated product.

*Likelihood: High (without deliberate brand direction). Impact: High.*

### Risk 4 — Voice Guide Too Vague to Execute
Brand voice guides frequently fail because they use adjectives ("authentic," "warm," "thoughtful") that sound right but could describe any brand. A voice guide that says "Rise Jars is warm and knowledgeable" gives a copywriter no usable direction. Without specific "sounds like / doesn't sound like" sentence examples, the voice guide is decoration, not direction.

*Likelihood: High (without Atlas-level specificity). Impact: Medium.*

### Risk 5 — Brand Identity System Not Delivered to Downstream Teams
Brand identity documents are valuable only if they are distributed and used. Rise Jars may produce an excellent Brand Identity System and then have it sit unused when a freelance copywriter, packaging designer, or retailer requests assets. Atlas must track Brand Identity System distribution as part of Stage 8 completion, and Stage 11 must reference the system explicitly.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Medium.*

### Risk 6 — Long-Term Brand Architecture Not Considered
Rise Jars' long-term vision includes canisters, pantry containers, and a full kitchen storage ecosystem. If the brand identity is architected only for the launch jar product — with "Jars" baked into the name and identity — expansion will require either brand stretching (risky) or a sub-brand architecture (complex). This is not a blocker in Stage 8, but it must be acknowledged and documented so that future brand decisions are made with the architecture in mind.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Medium.*

# Stage 9 — Intellectual Property

---

## 1. Stage Overview

### Purpose

Stage 9 protects Rise Jars' competitive advantage before it becomes visible in the market. By the time marketing begins (Stage 11), Rise Jars will be showing its product publicly — at which point unprotected IP is vulnerable. Stage 9 secures that protection first: provisional or utility patent to protect the modular mechanism, trademark to protect the brand name, design patent to protect the visual form, trade secret documentation to protect manufacturing processes, and NDAs to protect every relationship that involves disclosure.

This is not a founder-driven legal task. Atlas manages the preparation work — the IP Brief, the filing checklist, the NDA templates, the IP Status Tracker — and an IP attorney handles the legal execution. The founder's job is to engage the right attorney and make a small number of strategic decisions Atlas cannot make for them.

### Primary Objective

Deliver an **attorney-ready IP Brief** that answers every question a competent IP attorney will ask in the first meeting, initiate at least one IP protection action (provisional patent filing, trademark filing, or formal trade secret documentation), execute NDAs with all manufacturing partners identified in Stage 7, and initialize the IP Status Tracker that will serve as the living IP record for all remaining Atlas stages.

### Success Definition

Success in Stage 9 means Rise Jars can enter Stage 11 (Marketing) with "patent pending" status and trademark-pending status — both of which are marketing assets — and can enter Stage 13 (Funding) with a clean IP status report that satisfies investor due diligence. Every manufacturer and contractor who has seen Rise Jars' design has signed an NDA. The IP Status Tracker is initialized and current.

---

## 2. What Rise Jars Needs

Rise Jars has specific IP needs that are different from a software product or a simple consumer good:

- **Utility patent for the modular mechanism.** The stackable architecture — specifically the mechanism that allows jars of the same diameter to stack stably, and the universal lid system that eliminates lid chaos — is Rise Jars' primary functional innovation. This is the utility patent candidate. The claim language must capture the mechanism, not just the product form.

- **Design patent for the visual form.** The jar shape, lid profile, and system aesthetic are design patent candidates. Design patents are faster, cheaper, and more reliably granted than utility patents. For a consumer product with strong visual differentiation, a design patent portfolio is a meaningful protection layer.

- **Trademark registration for "Rise Jars."** Stage 4 produced a Trademark Clearance Brief; Stage 8 completed the full trademark search. Stage 9 files the application with the USPTO in Class 21 (kitchen utensils and household articles of glass, porcelain, and earthenware).

- **Trade secret documentation for the borosilicate sourcing relationship.** If Rise Jars has identified specific glass suppliers or developed preferred formulations, those relationships are trade secrets. Formal documentation protects them.

- **NDAs for all manufacturing partners.** Stage 7 engaged with glass manufacturers and lid/gasket component suppliers. All of those relationships must have NDA coverage before Stage 9 can close. If NDAs were not in place during Stage 7 (a common oversight), they must be executed now, retroactively covering all disclosures made.

- **IP Brief for attorney meeting.** The IP Brief is the foundational deliverable. A well-prepared IP Brief saves multiple hours of attorney time, reduces legal costs, and ensures the attorney files with full context rather than a generic brief.

---

## 3. Questions Atlas Should Ask

1. **Has any information about Rise Jars' design — the stacking mechanism, the lid system, the jar form — been disclosed publicly?** *(This determines whether a public disclosure has started the 12-month statutory bar clock in the US, which affects provisional vs. utility patent urgency.)*

2. **Have all manufacturing contacts from Stage 7 signed NDAs?** *(If not, this is an immediate action item before proceeding. Atlas cannot assume NDAs are in place.)*

3. **Which aspect of Rise Jars' design do you most want to protect — the mechanism that makes the stacking work, the visual form of the jar, the brand name, or all three?** *(Surfaces where the founder places greatest IP value and ensures the patent strategy is founder-aligned.)*

4. **Do you have a preference between filing a provisional patent now (lower cost, 12-month placeholder, "patent pending" immediately) versus going straight to a utility patent (higher cost, longer process, full protection)?** *(Atlas recommends based on product development stage, but this is a financial and strategic decision the founder makes.)*

5. **Are you planning to sell or license Rise Jars to another company within the next 3–5 years — or is this a brand you intend to own and build long-term?** *(Exit strategy affects the IP portfolio strategy: a broad patent portfolio increases acquisition value but increases cost.)*

6. **Have you worked with an IP attorney before, or is this your first time engaging one?** *(Affects how Atlas structures the IP Brief and attorney briefing — first-time founders need more framing; experienced ones need less.)*

7. **Are there any existing relationships — co-developers, consultants, industrial design firms — who contributed to the Rise Jars design and who might have IP claims?** *(Invention assignment agreements may be needed; this is critical to surface before filing.)*

8. **Do you have international distribution ambitions in the first 2–3 years — specifically EU, UK, Canada, or Australia?** *(International IP protection adds cost but is necessary if distribution is planned; PCT filing strategy must be considered alongside US filing.)*

9. **Is the specific borosilicate glass formulation or the sourcing relationship with a specific glass supplier information you want to keep confidential — or is it publicly discoverable?** *(Determines whether trade secret documentation for the supply chain is needed.)*

10. **What is your budget for IP protection in Year 1?** *(Directly shapes the IP strategy: provisional + trademark + design patent can be managed under $5,000 with attorney costs; utility patent adds $8,000–$15,000.)*

## 11. Risks Discovered

### Risk 1 — 12-Month Statutory Bar: Public Disclosure Before Filing
If any information about Rise Jars' design has been publicly disclosed — at a trade show, in a podcast interview, on social media, in a crowdfunding campaign preview — the 12-month statutory bar for US utility patent filing begins from that date. If a provisional patent is not filed within 12 months of first public disclosure, the right to file a utility patent is permanently lost. Atlas must surface this risk clearly and calculate the deadline if any public disclosure has occurred.

*Likelihood: Medium (many founders disclose casually before understanding the implications). Impact: Critical.*

Stage 7 involved engaging with glass manufacturers and lid/gasket suppliers, likely including factory tours, design reviews, and sample requests. ## 1. Stage Overview

### Purpose

Stage 10 establishes the price Rise Jars will charge — not by picking a number that feels right, but by applying three structured analytical frameworks that triangulate the correct price from three directions simultaneously: what it costs to make and deliver (the floor), what competitors charge (the context), and what the customer gains from buying (the ceiling). The resulting price is defensible, margin-positive, and grounded in customer value rather than founder intuition or cost-plus guessing.

Pricing is established before marketing and sales because the price point determines the marketing message, the sales channel strategy, the retailer margin math, and the break-even unit target. Every downstream stage needs a confirmed price.

### Primary Objective

Produce a **Pricing Strategy Document** for Rise Jars containing: confirmed recommended retail price, full unit economics cascade (revenue → COGS → gross margin → channel margin → net margin), break-even analysis, three pricing scenario comparisons, and pricing architecture decision — all built on the three-framework sequence of cost-plus floor, competitive benchmarking, and value-based ceiling.

### Success Definition

Success in Stage 10 means Rise Jars has a confirmed price it can defend in every context: to a retailer negotiating margin, to an investor asking about unit economics, to a customer asking why the product costs more than OXO, and to the founder reviewing whether the business is viable. The price is not a guess — it is a conclusion reached through explicit analysis, with documented reasoning that can be updated as costs change.

## 2. What Rise Jars Needs

Rise Jars enters Stage 10 with more relevant prior work than most founders realize:

- **Unit Economics Model from Stage 7.** The manufacturing quotes and cost-per-unit figures from the RFQ process are the cost-plus floor inputs. This model exists. Atlas does not need to reconstruct it — it needs to plug it into the pricing framework.

- **Customer segment profiles with willingness-to-pay signals.** The customer research from Stages 2 and 3 surfaced price sensitivity and value perception data for the primary and secondary segments. The health-conscious meal-prep buyer has demonstrated willingness to invest in premium kitchen products — this is the relevant evidence for the value-based ceiling analysis.

- **Competitive pricing data.** Stage 2/3 established that OXO glass sets are priced at $25–$40, Pyrex sets at $15–$30, and Weck jars (premium/specialty) at $40–$80 for sets. Rise Jars at $80–$150 is a deliberate premium over all of these. The competitive benchmarking framework confirms the premium distance and the argument for it.

- **Brand positioning from Stage 8.** A Sage/Caregiver lifestyle brand at premium positioning directly supports the upper end of the $80–$150 range. Brand positioning is not a soft input to pricing — it is a hard one. A brand that communicates premium justifies premium pricing. A brand that doesn't, cannot.

- **Distribution channel plan.** Rise Jars' distribution plan (DTC primary → specialty retail → Amazon) has different margin structures. A DTC sale at $120 has very different net economics than a Williams-Sonoma sale at $120 wholesale. The pricing model must show both.

**The specific pricing problem Rise Jars faces:**
The 3–5× premium over OXO and Pyrex is the central pricing challenge. Every pricing analysis must articulate the premium rationale in customer-facing language — not internal cost reasoning, but customer value language. The customer is not paying more because borosilicate glass costs more to produce. They are paying more because borosilicate glass can go from freezer to oven without breaking, will never leach plasticizers, will still look beautiful on an open shelf five years from now, and is part of a modular system that eliminates the chaos of mismatched lids. That value story is the ceiling argument.

## 3. Questions Atlas Should Ask

1. **What is your minimum acceptable gross margin at the manufacturer level, before channel margins are applied?** *(The single most important number in pricing. Without this, the cost-plus floor analysis cannot produce a floor — it produces a range. The founder must give Atlas a number.)*

2. **If a customer asks you why Rise Jars costs $120 when they can buy a Pyrex set for $30, what do you say?** *(This is the value-based pricing question in disguise. The founder's natural answer is the customer value proposition that drives the ceiling analysis.)*

3. **Are you pricing the set (multiple jars) or individual jars — and what is the planned SKU configuration for the initial launch?** *(Pricing architecture question. 9. **What is your cost-per-unit at the initial manufacturing volume (100–500 units) and at your target scale volume (5,000–10,000 units)?** *(This data should be in the Stage 7 Unit Economics Model — Atlas asks this as a confirmation question to ensure the model is current.)*

10. **Is there a price below which you would not sell Rise Jars, regardless of the business analysis — because it would feel inconsistent with the brand?** *(Brand floor question — the founder may have a visceral floor that is higher than the cost-plus floor. This is a legitimate constraint.)*

- Fixed costs for Year 1 (tooling amortization, IP costs, marketing budget, overhead)
- Break-even unit volume at recommended price (DTC and retail channels separately)
- Break-even revenue at recommended price
- Months to break-even at projected monthly sales rate
- Sensitivity analysis: break-even if actual volume is 25% lower / 25% higher than projected

### 6.4 Pricing Scenario Analysis
Three complete scenarios (lower / recommended / higher):
- Scenario A — Lower Price: one specific price at the lower bound of the justified range
- Scenario B — Recommended Price: Atlas's recommended price
- Scenario C — Higher Price: one specific price at the upper bound of the justified range

For each scenario:
- Gross margin
- Net margin (DTC channel)
- Break-even volume (units/year)
- Brand alignment assessment (does this price point fit the confirmed brand positioning?)
- Risk assessment (what breaks at this price — too low: margin insufficient; too high: market resistance)

### 6.5 Competitive Price Positioning Map
A visual-ready (table format for Atlas output) comparison:
- Each competitor with price, product description, channel, and gross margin estimate (where public)
- Rise Jars at recommended price positioned relative to each
- Premium distance and value justification narrative for each price gap
- "Why Rise Jars costs more than [X]" customer-ready language for each key competitor

## 8. Readiness Evaluation

### Not Ready
Rise Jars is Not Ready to exit Stage 10 if any of the following are true:
- Recommended retail price has NOT been confirmed by the founder
- Gross margin at the recommended price does NOT meet the founder's stated minimum margin target — meaning the product cannot be viably sold at this price
- Stage 7 Unit Economics Model is not available or was never completed — the cost-plus floor cannot be calculated without COGS data
- Break-even analysis has not been completed — the founder does not know how many units must sell for the business to break even
- Pricing architecture is undecided — it is unclear whether Rise Jars will be priced as a set, individually, with DTC/retail pricing parity, or with a different structure

### Getting There
Rise Jars is Getting There if:
- All three pricing frameworks have been applied but the recommended price has not been confirmed by the founder
- Gross margin at the tentative price is close to but not confirmed to meet the minimum margin target
- Break-even analysis is drafted but not finalized (some inputs are estimates rather than confirmed figures)
- Pricing architecture has a recommendation from Atlas but has not been confirmed
- The Pricing Strategy Document is in draft form and in founder review
- The DTC/retail channel comparison model exists but the primary channel decision is pending

### Ready to Move Forward
Rise Jars is Ready to Move Forward when:
- All three pricing frameworks have been applied and documented
- Gross margin at recommended price meets or exceeds founder's stated minimum margin target
- Break-even analysis is complete with confirmed inputs
- Pricing architecture is decided: SKU configuration, set vs. individual pricing, DTC vs. retail pricing structure
- Pricing Strategy Document is confirmed by founder
- Unit Economics Model (complete) is confirmed and matches Stage 7 COGS inputs
- Three pricing scenarios are documented with gross margin and break-even implications
- Primary Year 1 channel decision is confirmed and the channel economics model validates the choice

7. **Channel economics documented.** Unit economics at recommended price for DTC, specialty retail, and Amazon channels — all calculated and reviewed.

## 11. Risks Discovered

### Risk 1 — Cost-Plus Floor Exceeds Market Expectations
Borosilicate glass is genuinely more expensive than soda-lime glass. Combined with the modular lid system's tooling and component cost, the initial COGS may be higher than expected — pushing the cost-plus floor above what the market has shown it will pay. If the cost-plus floor at initial volume is $70+/set, the recommended retail price at standard CPG gross margins (50–60%) would need to be $140–$175 — potentially above the $80–$150 price intuition. This is the most important risk to surface in Stage 10: the product may need to reach scale volume before the unit economics support a viable premium price.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Critical.*

### Risk 2 — Price Compression from Asian DTC Competitors
The cache notes a specific risk: Amazon private-label and Asian DTC manufacturers could produce a $35 borosilicate glass set within 12–18 months of Rise Jars' market launch success. This is not hypothetical — it is the pattern for every successful premium CPG innovation in the kitchen/housewares category. Once Rise Jars proves the market at $80–$150, copycats will arrive. The pricing strategy must account for this: Rise Jars' moat must be brand + system design + community, not product specification alone.

*Likelihood: High (within 12–24 months of launch success). Impact: High.*

### Risk 3 — Specialty Retail Margin Math Breaks the Model
Williams-Sonoma and Sur La Table typically require 50–60% wholesale discount off MSRP. If Rise Jars' MSRP is $120 and Williams-Sonoma takes 50%, Rise Jars receives $60 per set. If COGS is $35 at initial volume, the gross margin is $25/set — which may be below the founder's minimum margin target and may be insufficient to fund marketing and operations. The retail channel economics may not work until Rise Jars reaches scale volume. Atlas should surface this clearly: specialty retail may not be viable at launch volume and may need to wait until COGS drops at scale.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: High.*

### Risk 4 — Founder Anchors on Intuition Price Rather Than Framework Price
The $80–$150 price intuition was formed in Stage 1 — before manufacturing cost data, before brand positioning, and before customer value quantification. Founders frequently anchor to their first price intuition even when the framework analysis produces a different result. Atlas must present the framework conclusions before asking the founder to confirm a price — not ask the founder to confirm $80–$150 before running the analysis.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Medium.*

### Risk 5 — No Pricing Architecture for System Expansion
Rise Jars' long-term vision includes canisters, pantry containers, and a full kitchen storage ecosystem. The pricing architecture established at launch will be difficult to change later. If launch pricing does not account for how the full system will be priced — individual components vs. sets, subscription for accessories, bundle pricing for the full system — the pricing architecture may need to be rebuilt when expansion products launch.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Medium.*

### Risk 6 — Break-Even Volume Requires Faster Growth Than Distribution Plan Supports
At a premium price and specialty retail margins, the break-even volume may require 2,000–5,000 units in Year 1 to cover fixed costs. If the primary Year 1 channel is DTC (typically lower volume than retail for a new brand without an existing audience), the break-even timeline may be 18–24 months — which affects the funding story in Stage 13. Atlas should surface the break-even timeline explicitly so the founder can calibrate expectations before the investor pitch.

*Likelihood: Medium. Impact: High.*

### Opportunity 1 — Automated Unit Economics Builder
Atlas should be able to construct the complete unit economics model from the Stage 7 inputs (COGS components from the RFQ process) without the founder manually re-entering the data. *Priority: Must Have*

*Priority: Must Have*

*Priority: High*

### Opportunity 6 — Break-Even Timeline Visualizer
Atlas should present break-even analysis as a timeline — "at your projected monthly DTC volume of 200 units, you will break even in month 14" — not just as a static unit count. Founders understand timelines more intuitively than unit thresholds, and a timeline connects to the funding conversation in Stage 13.

*Priority: Should Have*

# Founder Notes — "I Wish Atlas Could Do This"

## Must Have

### 1. Brand Name Generator
When "Rise Jars" fails trademark clearance, the founder has no naming tool. Atlas should generate brand name candidates based on archetype, positioning territory, category keywords, customer emotional register, syllable count criteria, and built-in trademark risk filtering. Without this, founders with a naming problem have nowhere to go.

*Stage: 8*

### 2. IP Brief Auto-Assembler
The IP Brief is the most important Stage 9 output — and it is 80% buildable from prior stage data (product description from Stage 5, prior art from Stage 4, novel elements from Stage 4, brand information from Stage 8). Atlas should assemble this automatically, leaving only gap-fill questions for the founder. Currently, founders build it from scratch or skip it, meaning attorneys get incomplete briefs.

*Stage: 9*

### 3. NDA Generator
Atlas should be able to generate a complete, jurisdiction-appropriate NDA for a US manufacturing partner — not a template with blanks, but a finished document ready for attorney review and execution. For Rise Jars, this means an NDA that specifically covers the borosilicate glass stacking mechanism, modular lid architecture, and supplier relationships.

*Stage: 9*

### 4. Cost-Plus Pricing Calculator
Atlas should automatically ingest the Stage 7 Unit Economics Model and calculate the cost-plus floor at the founder's stated minimum margin — showing the minimum viable retail price and how it changes with different gross margin targets. This is pure math that founders currently do manually (often incorrectly).

*Stage: 10*

### 5. Unit Economics Dashboard
A live, interactive unit economics model showing COGS → gross margin → channel margin → net margin at any given price point and volume — with DTC, retail, and Amazon calculated side-by-side. Founders need to see these numbers as a connected system, not in separate spreadsheets.

*Stage: 10*

### 6. Provisional Patent Deadline Tracker with Automated Alerts
Once a provisional patent filing date is entered, Atlas should automatically calculate the 12-month conversion deadline, display it prominently in the Atlas dashboard, and send alerts at 9 months, 11 months, and 11.5 months. *Stage: 9*
