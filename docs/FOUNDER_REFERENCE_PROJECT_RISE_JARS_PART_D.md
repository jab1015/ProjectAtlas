## SECTION 1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Founder Reference Project for Rise Jars is the most comprehensive stress test Atlas has undergone. Over four parts and 15 stages, Atlas was evaluated as the sole guide for a real-world physical consumer goods product — a premium borosilicate glass food storage jar system — from initial idea through commercial growth.

**The Core Finding:**

Atlas is a structurally sound foundation that exhibits genuine value in the stages it has built but has critical infrastructure gaps that prevent it from fulfilling its own promise in physical, hardware, and CPG invention categories. Today Atlas operates between Level 1 (Guided) and Level 2 (Assisted) of the Automation Maturity Model. The target is Level 3 (Autonomous) within 12 months.

**What Atlas Gets Right:**
- The core promise — "Atlas Owns Execution" — is architecturally encoded correctly. The engine-owns-progress pattern (ADR-001), three-state readiness (ADR-002), and configuration-driven journey (ADR-003) are the right foundations.
- Live stages (1–4) demonstrate genuine value: structured idea extraction, validation planning, market sizing framework, patent readiness guidance. A founder with Atlas is meaningfully better off than a founder without it.
- The "assemble, don't regenerate" principle is Atlas's highest-value pattern. The pitch deck assembled 80%+ from prior stages, the sales toolkit built from messaging architecture, the IP brief pulled from design and patent stages — this compounds value dramatically.
- The Automation Constitution, when followed, is the right operating framework. Every gap found in this project is a gap where Atlas departed from Constitution principles.

**What Atlas Must Fix:**
1. **Analytics Integration** — Without connecting to Shopify, GA4, email platforms, and ad platforms, Atlas's commercial stages (11–15) are planning-only. This is the single highest-priority infrastructure gap.
2. **Physical Product Module** — Atlas is architecturally software-product-centric. Rise Jars exposed 14 critical gaps specific to physical CPG products: glass manufacturing, tooling costs, food contact compliance, multi-component supply chains, physical prototype paths, retail compliance requirements. A physical product vertical is required.
3. **Automated Research Engine** — Atlas still asks founders for information it should be finding itself: competitor pricing, prior art, market size, manufacturer options, retail buyer requirements. Each of these must become proactive Atlas output, not founder-supplied input.
4. **Document Auto-Assembly Pipeline** — The Constitution says "Generate Before Requesting." Too many stages still produce templates rather than finished, data-populated documents. IP briefs, pitch decks, sales toolkits, and unit economics models should all arrive pre-populated.

**The Strategic Finding:**

Atlas is one implementation cycle away from being genuinely transformative for inventors. The architecture is correct. The principles are right. The gaps are well-defined. The question is not whether Atlas can do this — it is whether the next build cycle prioritizes the gaps that most directly reduce inventor failure rates.

**Rise Jars Overall Assessment:** If an inventor built Rise Jars with Atlas today, they would be significantly better organized than the average inventor, but would face 14 critical gaps that could each individually stall or kill the project. Atlas dramatically increases their probability of reaching Stages 1–4 outcomes. It is not yet reliable through Stages 5–15.

## SECTION 5 — FOUNDER DECISION MAP

*For all 15 stages: what Atlas does vs. what the founder does.*

### Universal Rules

**Atlas Always Does:**
Research • Generate first draft of every document • Calculate (unit economics, market sizing, break-even, margins) • Compare (competitors, manufacturers, channels) • Monitor (KPIs, inventory, patent status, competitive activity, pricing) • Recommend with reasoning and trade-offs • Auto-populate downstream documents from upstream data • Alert on risks and deadlines

**Founder Always Does:**
Final yes/no on all spending and strategic decisions • Physical presence (testing, relationship meetings, negotiations) • Legal decisions and signatures • Creative vision and final aesthetic judgment • Relationship formation (investors, retailers, influencers, manufacturers) • Personal story and team narrative • Brand voice fine-tuning from Atlas draft

## SECTION 10 — ATLAS SELF-EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

### The 7-Question Pre-Stage Hook

Before every stage begins, Atlas must run through a self-evaluation that produces the stage's action plan. This evaluation is never shown to the founder as a checklist — it is an internal engine behavior that shapes what Atlas does automatically before the founder sees anything.

**Question 1: What do I already know?**
What data has Atlas already collected from prior stages that is relevant to this stage? No question should be asked that Atlas can answer from prior stage data. Every prior stage output should be mapped to current stage inputs.

*Example — Stage 10 (Pricing):* Atlas already has COGS from Stage 7 manufacturer quotes, competitive landscape from Stage 3, brand positioning from Stage 8, and distribution channels from Stage 11 planning. Unit economics model can be auto-populated before the founder opens Stage 10.

**Question 2: What am I assuming?**
What assumptions is Atlas making in its analysis? Every assumption must be flagged explicitly and confirmed with the founder or verified with research before being used in final outputs.

*Example — Stage 7 (Manufacturing):* Atlas is assuming single-geography manufacturing. Rise Jars has glass, lid, and gasket components potentially from different countries. Atlas should surface this assumption and verify component-level manufacturing strategy.

**Question 3: What can I research?**
What publicly available information can Atlas gather before asking the founder anything? Competitive pricing, prior art, manufacturer options, market size, retail buyer requirements, regulatory thresholds — all are findable before the first founder interaction.

*Example — Stage 4 (Patent Readiness):* Atlas can run prior art searches, surface the top 10 most relevant patents, identify the clearest prior art risks, and produce a gap analysis before the founder opens the stage.

**Question 4: What can I automate?**
What work would the founder otherwise do manually that Atlas can complete automatically? Document drafting, calculations, research, pipeline generation, competitive comparisons — every automatable task should be completed before the founder arrives.

*Example — Stage 13 (Funding):* Atlas can auto-populate 80%+ of the pitch deck from prior stages, generate the 3-year financial model from unit economics, and produce the Investor FAQ with answers pulled from the invention record — all before the founder opens Stage 13.

**Question 5: What should I generate?**
What documents should be ready when the founder opens this stage? Atlas should never hand the founder a blank template. Every document should arrive pre-populated with Atlas's best available draft.

*Example — Stage 11 (Marketing):* When Stage 11 opens, Atlas delivers: channel evaluation matrix pre-scored, messaging architecture first draft, landing page copy draft, 90-day content calendar, and influencer research list — all ready for founder review.

**Question 6: What risks should I warn about?**
What could go wrong in this stage that the founder may not know to look for? Surface risks proactively. Don't wait for the founder to discover them.

*Example — Stage 9 (IP):* Atlas should proactively flag: (1) if a provisional patent filing date implies the 12-month window is approaching, (2) if any public disclosures were logged and the statutory bar date is within 90 days, (3) if manufacturer NDAs haven't been signed before design sharing has occurred.

**Question 7: Can I eliminate work for the founder?**
After running Questions 1–6, what work remains for the founder? Is any of it work Atlas could take on with reasonable quality? The goal is to reduce founder work to: strategic decisions, physical presence, relationship formation, legal signatures, and creative judgment.

### How This Becomes Permanent Engine Behavior

The self-evaluation framework should be encoded as a stage lifecycle hook in `convex/journeyEngine.ts`. Implementation recommendation:

**Architecture:**

```
stageConfig[n].onOpen = async (ctx, inventionId) => {
  await runStagePreEvaluation(ctx, inventionId, stageConfig[n])
}
```

`runStagePreEvaluation` executes the 7-question framework against:
- All prior stage outputs stored in `stageProgress` table
- External research APIs (web search, USPTO, pricing feeds)
- Atlas's document generation pipeline

**Stage Open Trigger Sequence:**
1. Stage gates check passes (prior stage complete, tier allows)
2. Pre-evaluation runs (background, 30–60 seconds)
3. Atlas populates stage with research results, auto-generated drafts, pre-filled data
4. Founder opens stage and finds: documents drafted, data populated, risks surfaced, recommendations present
5. Founder reviews, approves, and makes decisions — does not build from scratch

## SECTION 13 — FINAL VERDICT

### What Atlas Would Build Differently

If Atlas were rebuilt from scratch today using everything learned from the Rise Jars project:

**1. Physical product vertical from Day 1.**
The implicit software-product assumption runs so deep in the current architecture that it surfaces in every Stage 5–10 gap. The next atlas should know on Day 1 whether an inventor is building software, a physical product, a service, or a platform — and activate the appropriate vertical module immediately. This is a single configuration change that unlocks an entirely different product experience.

**2. Research Engine before Journey Engine.**
The current architecture leads with the journey flow. The next Atlas would lead with the research engine. Every stage opens with Atlas having already done the work: competitors researched, prior art searched, pricing benchmarked, manufacturers identified. The journey is the presentation layer for research that Atlas has already completed.

**3. Document pipeline as first-class infrastructure.**
The Constitution says "Generate Before Requesting." This principle should be encoded at the infrastructure level — not a feature on the roadmap but the default behavior of every stage. Stage opens → Atlas generates documents → founder reviews → never blank pages.

**4. Commercial stages with live data from Day 1.**
The analytics integration should not be a Phase 4 initiative. It should be part of the initial commercial stage architecture. Stage 14 and Stage 15 are not useful without live data. Building them without the integration is building a car without an engine.

**5. Risk tracking as a permanent sidebar element.**
IP deadlines, public disclosures, statutory bars, manufacturing NDAs, patent filing status — these risks don't live in one stage. They persist across the entire journey. They should live in a permanent risk tracker visible at all times, not buried in a single stage.

### What Should Never Change

**1. The Automation Constitution.**
The principles are correct. Every gap found in this project is a place where Atlas departed from them. The Constitution should be enforced architecturally, not aspirationally.

**2. Engine-Owns-Progress (ADR-001).**
The separation between computation (journeyEngine.ts) and presentation (UI) is the right architecture. It enables Atlas to evolve the readiness model without breaking the interface.

**3. Three-State Readiness (ADR-002).**
Not Ready / Getting There / Ready to Move Forward is the right UX contract. It removes false precision, reduces founder anxiety, and communicates clearly. The internal 0–100 score should remain hidden.

**4. Configuration-Driven Journey (ADR-003).**
One flag enables a stage. This architecture enables rapid iteration, A/B testing, and vertical-specific configuration without architectural changes.

**5. The "Assemble, Don't Regenerate" Pattern.**
Pitch deck assembled from 8 prior stage documents. Sales toolkit built from messaging architecture. IP brief constructed from design and patent stages. This pattern is Atlas's highest-value behavior and should be systematically applied to every document in every stage.

**6. The Founder Decision Map.**
Judgment, creativity, vision, relationships, negotiation, physical testing, legal decisions — these belong to the founder. Everything else belongs to Atlas. This division is correct and must never erode. The moment Atlas starts asking founders to do research Atlas should be doing, or asking questions Atlas can answer, or handing founders blank templates instead of completed drafts — Atlas is failing its own mission.

### Permanent Principles

**Principle 1 — Atlas Owns Execution.**
If Atlas can do it, Atlas does it. Before asking. Without being told. Every time.

**Principle 2 — The Founder's Job Is Judgment.**
Atlas researches. Atlas generates. Atlas calculates. Atlas recommends. The founder decides. The founder signs. The founder builds relationships. The founder tests physically. Everything else is Atlas's job.

**Principle 3 — No Blank Pages.**
Every document Atlas knows needs to exist should be drafted before the founder sees the stage. Atlas's job is to eliminate "I don't know where to start" from every inventor's experience.

**Principle 4 — Honest About Probability, Silent About Prediction.**
Atlas maximizes the probability of success. Atlas does not predict success. Atlas does not promise outcomes. Atlas does not imply that following the process guarantees results. The market, investors, manufacturers, retailers, and regulators make their own decisions. Atlas's promise is thorough preparation, not guaranteed arrival.

**Principle 5 — Risk Before Regret.**
Atlas surfaces every identified risk before the decision that makes it irreversible. IP deadlines before trade shows. Tooling costs before SKU commitment. MOQ implications before manufacturing contracts. Price acceptance uncertainty before marketing investment. The best time to surface a risk is before the founder can do something irreversible. The second best time is immediately after.

**Principle 6 — Data Compounds.**
Every piece of data collected in Stage 1 should still be active in Stage 15. The pitch deck knows the problem because Stage 1 captured it. The pricing strategy knows the competitive landscape because Stage 3 researched it. The IP brief knows the novel elements because Stage 4 documented them. Nothing should ever be asked twice. Everything compounds.

**Principle 7 — Build for the Inventor Who Doesn't Know What They Don't Know.**
The most valuable Atlas user is not a seasoned entrepreneur who knows the path. It is the first-time inventor who has an idea, limited resources, and no guide. Every Atlas decision should optimize for this founder. Assume they don't know about the 12-month statutory bar. Assume they don't know what an MOQ is. Assume they don't know what a keystone margin means. Explain. Warn. Guide. Never assume the founder knows what Atlas hasn't yet taught them.

**Atlas Honest Assessment:**

Rise Jars is a genuinely good product idea with a real target market, authentic differentiation, and defensible IP. A founder using Atlas today would be meaningfully more prepared than one without it. They would know their market, understand their competitive landscape, have a documented IP strategy, and arrive at manufacturing with a real product specification.

They would also face a $40K–$125K tooling decision without Atlas cost modeling it, a 12-month patent deadline without Atlas tracking it, a $80–$150 price point without Atlas testing it, and a launch day without Atlas monitoring it.

Atlas is one serious build cycle away from being the operating system Rise Jars needs. The architecture is right. The principles are right. The gaps are well-defined. The question is whether the next 12 months are spent closing those gaps systematically — or adding features that aren't blocked on the ones above.

The answer, according to everything learned from Rise Jars, is clear: close the gaps. The founders are waiting.
