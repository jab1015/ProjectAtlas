# Stage 5 — Product Design

## What Rise Jars Needs

Rise Jars enters Stage 5 with a conceptually complete idea but no engineering artifacts. The founder knows that jars should stack, seal airtight, and be made from borosilicate glass — but none of these concepts have been translated into engineering language. At Stage 5, the founder must accomplish the following:

**1. Define the complete jar line.** Rise Jars is a system, not a single product. How many SKUs are in the initial line? What are the volumes (e.g., 400ml, 800ml, 1200ml, 1800ml)? What is the diameter-to-height ratio for each size? Does the initial line cover all primary use cases (small herbs, medium leftovers, large batch meal prep)? These decisions must be made before any engineering work begins, because the modular system constraint — that lids are interchangeable and jars stack — must be satisfied across the entire line simultaneously.

**2. Nail the universal lid architecture.** The "modular universal lid system" is Rise Jars' most structurally differentiated feature and also the hardest engineering problem. A single lid that fits multiple jar diameters is mechanically complex. The founder must decide whether the universality is at the rim level (one rim diameter across all sizes, varying only in height) or at the lid level (a lid with an adjustable seating mechanism). This decision constrains everything: jar proportions, stacking geometry, and manufacturing tooling.

**3. Define the stacking geometry precisely.** "Stackable" is not a specification. The founder needs to define exactly how jars of the same diameter stack — whether the base of one jar nests into the lid of the jar below (lid-as-platform design), whether jars stack base-to-base with a separate stacking ring, or whether the jar base has a recess that receives the lid ring. Each approach has different tooling implications, different stability properties, and different aesthetic results.

**4. Specify the sealing mechanism.** The airtight seal requires engineering specification: what type of gasket (silicone, EPDM, TPE), what durometer (softness/hardness), what profile (O-ring, custom molded), how it seats in the lid, and how the lid locks. The locking mechanism — clip, twist, press-fit — affects manufacturing complexity and user experience. This decision cannot be deferred to prototyping without a defined starting specification.

**5. Specify borosilicate glass parameters.** Borosilicate glass is not a single specification — it is a family of glass compositions. The founder must define (with specialist input if needed): glass grade (Type I vs. Type 3.3 borosilicate), wall thickness, thermal tolerance range, and whether the glass body is pressed or blown (which affects cost, surface quality, and dimensional consistency). These are not cosmetic decisions — they determine which manufacturers can produce the product.

**6. Define the visual and aesthetic specification.** The premium visual language of Rise Jars — proportions, shoulder geometry, base profile, lid finish, surface texture or optical clarity — must be documented in the design specification. This is not decoration; it is what differentiates the product aesthetically and forms the basis for any design patent filing.

**7. Address food contact material compliance.** Every material in the product that contacts food must be identified: borosilicate glass (FDA food contact compliant by classification), the gasket material (must be FDA 21 CFR food-contact compliant), any lid coating or surface treatment (must be food-safe). This is a completion criterion, not a future-stage concern.

## Questions Atlas Should Ask

1. You've described a "universal lid system." Does every jar in the Rise Jars line share the same rim diameter — so one lid fits all sizes — or does the universality mean something different? Walk me through how a customer uses one lid across multiple jars.

2. When you say the jars are "stackable," what specifically supports the stack? Does the base of one jar rest on the lid of the jar below, is there a stacking ring or collar, or does the jar have a recessed base that captures the lid rim? Describe what the stacking assembly looks like from the side.

3. You've named borosilicate glass as the material. Which borosilicate specification are you targeting — Type 3.3 (the standard lab-grade borosilicate used in high-end cookware) or a different grade? Do you have a target wall thickness, or is that something a glass engineer needs to determine?

4. What sizes are in the initial Rise Jars line? List every SKU you're planning to launch with: what is the intended volume for each (in ml or oz), and what is the primary use case for each size — spice storage, meal prep portions, liquids, bulk dry goods?

5. Is the lid made from the same borosilicate glass as the jar body, or from a different material? If glass, is it pressed or blown? If another material, what is it — and what is the reasoning?

6. Describe the sealing mechanism to me at a component level. What holds the lid against the jar? Is there a clip, a latch, a quarter-turn twist, or a press-fit? And what is the gasket made from — silicone, rubber, or something else?

7. What is the thermal range you want Rise Jars to support? Specifically: what is the intended minimum temperature (freezer use), and what is the intended maximum temperature (oven use)? And is the lid system designed to be oven-safe as well, or should the lid be removed before oven use?

8. What visual design references have you already assembled — sketch, reference photos, products in the market you admire aesthetically, mood board images? What is the shape language you're aiming for — cylindrical with vertical walls, tapered, with a pronounced shoulder, with a visible base foot?

9. Does every jar in the line need to be the same diameter with varying heights, or are you designing multiple diameter families (small/medium/large diameter classes)? This affects whether the universal lid is truly one lid or is actually one lid per diameter class.

10. What happens to the stacking system when the lid is off? If a customer has a jar without a lid, does it still stack stably on a surface? Can an empty jar be stored inverted? These use-case details have real design implications.

11. Have you identified the food contact compliance requirements for the materials you're specifying? For glass: is it FDA food contact compliant by classification? For the gasket: which FDA 21 CFR regulation applies to the silicone or rubber you intend to use?

12. What is the weight target for each jar size in the line? Weight affects the customer's perception of quality but also affects shipping cost per unit and packaging requirements. Do you have a target weight range, or is weight purely a derivative of the glass specification?

13. Are any jars in the line intended to be microwave-safe, in addition to oven-safe? Borosilicate glass is microwave-safe, but metallic lid components are not — and this use case has direct implications for how the lid is specified.

14. What is the target retail price range for the smallest jar and for a complete set? I'm asking now because every design decision you make at Stage 5 has a cost implication — and the specification must be manufacturable at a price point that makes the business work.

## AI Guidance

**Layer-by-Layer Design Structuring**
Atlas should walk the founder through the three design layers (Functional, Physical, User Experience) with particular emphasis on the physical layer for Rise Jars. The Functional specification is relatively clear from Stage 1–4 work: store food, seal airtight, stack stably, withstand thermal extremes. The Physical specification is where most work is required. Atlas should not allow the founder to proceed past a design layer with open specifications — each layer must be substantially complete before the next begins.

**Manufacturing Implication Surfacing (Glass-Specific)**
Atlas must surface the specific manufacturing implications of borosilicate glass in real time during Stage 5. These include:
- Glass blowing vs. pressing: blown glass has more variation in wall thickness; pressed glass is more dimensionally consistent but has different aesthetic qualities
- Mold tooling for glass: glass molds are steel, expensive to cut, and changes are costly — the design must be right before tooling is cut
- Dimensional tolerances for glass are wider than for injection-molded plastic — the stacking geometry must accommodate glass tolerance ranges, not assume plastic-like precision
- Borosilicate glass is manufactured by a smaller set of factories than soda-lime glass — the design must be validated against what factories can actually produce, not just what is theoretically possible
- Wall thickness directly affects both material cost and thermal performance — thinner walls reduce weight and cost but also reduce thermal mass (which affects how quickly contents cool or heat)

**Universal Lid Trade-Off Analysis**
The universal lid concept is the product's most ambitious engineering claim. Atlas should surface this trade-off clearly: a truly universal lid (one lid fits all jar diameters) is mechanically very difficult — lids sized for different rim diameters cannot share a single sealing geometry. A "universal" lid system almost always means one lid per diameter class, with multiple sizes sharing one lid within a class. Atlas should ask the founder to clarify what "universal" means in Rise Jars' specific context and document the decision explicitly — because this decision constrains the entire jar geometry.

**Stacking Geometry Options**
Atlas should present the three most common physical stacking approaches for glass jars and their trade-offs:
1. **Lid-as-platform**: the lid has a flat or recessed top surface that the base of the next jar rests on. Simple, no additional components, but the stack is only stable when lids are on. **Trade-off**: the lid must be strong enough to bear the weight of a full jar above it; the base-to-lid interface must be slip-resistant.
2. **Stacking ring**: a separate collar or ring sits between jars, decoupling the stacking function from the lid. Allows empty jars to stack. **Trade-off**: an additional component to source, store, and potentially lose.
3. **Base recess**: the jar base has a recessed groove or ring that captures the lid rim or jar rim below it. The stack is self-locating. **Trade-off**: the recess in the base may create a cleaning challenge and affects the jar's ability to sit flat independently.

Atlas should ask the founder to select an approach, document the reasoning, and note the implications for tooling.

**Specialist Engagement Flags**
Atlas should flag the following as requiring specialist engagement before the specification is complete:
- **Glass engineer or materials specialist**: to specify the correct borosilicate grade, wall thickness, and glass formation process for the use case
- **Sealing engineer or gasket specialist**: to specify the gasket material, profile, and durometer for the airtight performance requirement
- **Industrial designer**: to translate the founder's aesthetic vision into dimensioned design drawings suitable for tooling
- **Food safety regulatory consultant**: to confirm material compliance for food contact

**Design Completeness Evaluation**
Atlas should evaluate specification completeness against a physical product standard, not a software standard. The completeness check for Rise Jars must specifically include: are all materials named with grades specified? Are all dimensions defined (even if as targets to be refined by the industrial designer)? Is the stacking geometry described at a mechanism level? Is the sealing mechanism described with component-level detail?

## Risks Discovered

**Universal Lid Engineering Complexity (Critical)**
A true universal lid — one lid that seals all jar sizes airtight — is mechanically extremely difficult with glass jars. The sealing geometry (gasket compression, lid seat) must be precisely matched to the rim diameter. Attempting to create one lid for multiple rim diameters will likely compromise seal quality on smaller or larger sizes. Atlas must surface this risk immediately and help the founder reach a workable definition of "universal" before any engineering work is done. If the founder commits to a universal lid concept that cannot be engineered, Stage 6 will fail.

**Glass Dimensional Tolerance Implications for Stacking (Critical)**
Borosilicate glass has inherently wider manufacturing tolerances than injection-molded plastic. A stacking system designed for plastic-level precision will not function reliably with glass. The stacking geometry must be designed with glass tolerance ranges in mind — the locating feature must accommodate the dimensional variation that glass manufacturing produces. A design that works at 0.1mm tolerance will fail with glass that varies by 0.5–1.0mm. This is a specialist engineering requirement, not something the founder can resolve alone.

**Tooling Cost Front-Loading (High)**
Glass manufacturing requires custom molds (steel tooling) for each jar size and lid. A five-size jar line with one lid requires six custom molds at minimum. Glass molds are expensive ($15,000–$40,000 per mold is a plausible range depending on complexity and geography), and changes after tooling is cut are very costly. Stage 5 must finalize the design before tooling investment is made — there is almost no ability to "iterate in production" with glass tooling. Atlas should make this front-loading risk explicit during design specification work.

**Oven-Safe Lid System Complexity (High)**
If Rise Jars' oven-safe claim includes the lid — not just the glass body — the lid material must withstand oven temperatures (260°C / 500°F for borosilicate glass). Standard silicone gaskets are oven-safe to approximately 230°C. Some clip and locking mechanisms (particularly those with plastic components) are not oven-safe. If the lid system cannot survive oven temperatures, the "oven-safe" use case requires lid removal before oven use, which must be clearly communicated to the customer and documented in the specification.

**Food Contact Compliance Gap (High)**
The gasket and lid materials must be FDA food contact compliant and must be certified by the manufacturer for food contact use. This is not the founder's assumption to make — it requires documentation from the material supplier or manufacturer. If the chosen gasket or lid material does not have food contact certification, the product cannot legally be sold for food storage in the US. Atlas must make this a hard completion criterion, not a future-stage item.

**Design Over-Complexity for Launch (Medium)**
The modular system concept risks becoming over-engineered at Stage 5. A four-size jar line with a universal lid, a stacking system, an airtight sealing mechanism, and premium aesthetics is already a complex manufacturing project. Adding features at Stage 5 (e.g., a nesting tray system, a pour spout lid variant) significantly increases tooling cost and launch timeline. Atlas should help the founder identify the minimum viable system for launch — the fewest SKUs and features that deliver the core value proposition — and defer additional complexity to a second product generation.

**IP Filing Before Manufacturer Engagement (Medium)**
As identified in Part A Stage 4, sharing the design specification with glass manufacturers before filing a provisional patent application creates prior art risk. The Stage 5 design specification may be sufficiently detailed to enable a provisional filing. Atlas should prompt the founder to complete the IP action recommended in Stage 4 before Stage 5's specification is shared with any third party — including prototypers and potential manufacturing partners.

**Lead Time Underestimation (Low at Stage 5, High at Stage 7)**
First-time founders frequently underestimate the timeline from a complete design specification to a manufactured product. For a borosilicate glass CPG product, the realistic timeline from finalized specification to first production samples is typically 6–12 months, accounting for tooling fabrication, sample runs, testing, and revisions. Atlas should introduce this reality at Stage 5 so the founder's launch planning in Stage 14 is grounded in realistic lead times.

## Opportunities to Improve Atlas

**Gap: No Glass Product Design Module**
The Stage 5 blueprint is written for a generic physical product. It does not address the specific constraints of glass product design: the relationship between glass formation process and dimensional precision, the interaction between wall thickness and thermal performance, the tooling economics of glass molds, or the food contact compliance requirements for glass and associated materials. Atlas needs a glass/ceramics CPG product design guidance module that surfaces these considerations as part of the design specification workflow.

**Gap: No SKU Architecture Decision Framework**
For a product line (not a single product), the Stage 5 blueprint provides no guidance on how to structure the initial SKU architecture — how many sizes, what volumes, what constraints. For Rise Jars, the modular system constraint (all sizes must work together as a system) makes SKU architecture decisions more complex than a simple lineup decision. Atlas should provide a decision framework for product line architecture: how many sizes to launch with, how to balance completeness against complexity, and how to prioritize SKUs by customer use case.

**Gap: No Physical Tolerance Documentation**
The current Stage 5 blueprint does not prompt the founder to document dimensional tolerances. For plastic or soft goods products this may be acceptable — but for glass, wood, metal, or ceramic products where manufacturing tolerances are significantly wider than plastic, tolerance documentation is a fundamental design specification requirement. Atlas Stage 5 should add a tolerances section to the Physical Specification layer, with guidance on what tolerance ranges are appropriate for each manufacturing process.

**Gap: No Tooling Cost Preview**
Stage 5 builds the Product Design Specification without surfacing tooling cost implications. For a glass jar product, the number of unique molds (one per jar size, one per lid) and the estimated cost range for each mold is a fundamental consideration in deciding the initial SKU count. A five-size line costs more than twice as much to tool as a two-size line. Atlas should generate a tooling cost estimate at Stage 5 completion based on the number of unique molds implied by the design specification, so the founder understands the capital commitment before Stage 7.

**Gap: No Food Contact Compliance Workflow**
For any product that contacts food, Atlas should have a food contact compliance workflow at Stage 5. This workflow would: identify all materials that contact food, generate a compliance checklist (FDA 21 CFR, EU Regulation 10/2011, California Prop 65), prompt the founder to obtain supplier documentation, and flag any material for which compliance cannot be confirmed. Currently this is entirely absent.

## What Rise Jars Needs

Rise Jars faces a specific prototyping challenge that Atlas must address honestly: borosilicate glass cannot be 3D printed, machined on a desktop CNC, or fabricated with standard prototyping methods. The manufacturing process for glass jars (glass blowing or pressing in a factory with a mold) requires tooling investment before a production-quality prototype can be made. This is a fundamental constraint that changes how the Rise Jars founder must approach prototyping.

**The Rise Jars Prototyping Reality:**
The founder cannot produce a production-representative glass prototype without engaging a glass manufacturer. The options are:

1. **3D-printed form mock-up**: Use 3D printing (FDM or SLA) to test form, proportions, stacking geometry, and ergonomics. This is inexpensive and fast, but the material is wrong (not glass) and the aesthetics are wrong. It answers form and ergonomics questions only — not seal, not thermal performance, not durability.

2. **Glass manufacturer sample run**: Engage a borosilicate glass manufacturer to produce a small sample run (typically 20–50 units) from production-equivalent tooling or near-production tooling. This is expensive ($3,000–$15,000 minimum depending on the manufacturer and the complexity), takes 8–16 weeks, and requires a substantially complete design specification. It answers all questions — but requires significant upfront commitment.

3. **Proxy material prototype**: Commission a machined prototype from a material that approximates glass properties (e.g., clear acrylic) to test form, dimensions, sealing geometry, and stacking. More expensive than 3D printing but better approximates the aesthetic and dimensional qualities of glass. Does not test thermal performance.

4. **Hybrid approach**: 3D-printed form mock-up for early iteration, followed by proxy-material machined prototype for sealing and stacking validation, followed by a glass manufacturer sample run for final validation. ## What Rise Jars Needs

Manufacturing a borosilicate glass jar product is meaningfully different from manufacturing injection-molded plastic, soft goods, or electronics. The founder needs Atlas to guide her through a manufacturing landscape that has specific characteristics she is unlikely to know:

**1. Borosilicate glass manufacturing is specialized.** Not all glass manufacturers work with borosilicate. The majority of commodity glass jar manufacturers (in China, Southeast Asia, Eastern Europe, and the US) work primarily with soda-lime glass. Borosilicate glass requires higher melting temperatures, different forming equipment, and different annealing processes. The Rise Jars founder must identify manufacturers who specifically work with borosilicate — a significantly smaller pool than the general glass container market.

**2. Glass jar manufacturing hubs by geography:**
- **China (Zhejiang, Jiangsu provinces)**: The largest volume glass manufacturing base. Many manufacturers work with borosilicate. Pricing is competitive. Lead times are long (10–16 weeks). IP protection is a legitimate concern — NDAs are important but not a complete protection.
- **Eastern Europe (Poland, Czech Republic, Germany)**: Strong borosilicate glass manufacturing tradition (Schott Glaswerke, for example, is German-origin). Higher cost than China but shorter lead times for European brands, stronger IP enforcement environment.
- **United States**: Very limited borosilicate glass jar manufacturing. Libbey and Anchor Hocking produce soda-lime glass. Kimble Chase (now DWK Life Sciences) produces laboratory borosilicate. Domestic borosilicate glass jar manufacturing at CPG scale is essentially unavailable in the US.

**3. Tooling economics for glass jars are front-loaded and expensive.** Unlike injection molded plastic (where a simple mold can be cut for $3,000–$8,000), glass jar molds are steel blanks precision-machined for the specific jar profile and are typically $8,000–$25,000 per mold for a production-equivalent tool at a reputable Chinese factory, and higher in Europe. A five-SKU Rise Jars line (five jar sizes plus one or more lid sizes) implies tooling investment of $50,000–$150,000 depending on the manufacturer and the complexity.

**4. Minimum order quantities for glass jars are typically higher than plastic.** MOQs at Chinese borosilicate glass factories typically range from 5,000–20,000 units per SKU per run. At a premium price point, this implies significant capital tied up in inventory. The founder must understand MOQ implications for working capital planning.

**5. The lid and gasket are sourced separately.** In most glass jar manufacturing relationships, the glass body is manufactured at a glass factory, the lid (if non-glass) is manufactured at a separate factory (metal stamping, injection molding, or wood turning depending on material), and the silicone or rubber gasket is manufactured at yet another factory. Rise Jars' multi-component product may require managing 2–3 manufacturing relationships for a single SKU. The RFQ and Unit Economics Model must account for all components.

**6. Assembly, packaging, and kitting may require a fourth partner.** If the glass body, lid, and gasket are manufactured separately, they must be assembled and packaged together. Some glass manufacturers offer assembly and packaging services. Others do not. The Rise Jars supply chain model must clarify where assembly and packaging happen and include that cost in the unit economics model.

## Risks Discovered

**Tooling Investment Front-Loading (Critical)**
Rise Jars' five-size glass jar line requires custom tooling for each unique mold. At $8,000–$25,000 per mold, the total tooling investment before a single production unit is made is $50,000–$150,000 or more. This is a major capital commitment that the founder must plan for — and it must be expended before revenue is generated. If funding is insufficient to cover both tooling and initial inventory, the business cannot launch. Atlas must ensure this capital requirement is modeled explicitly and present in the Stage 13 (Funding) financial model.

**Borosilicate Glass Manufacturer Pool Is Small (High)**
The pool of manufacturers who specialize in borosilicate glass is significantly smaller than the pool who work with soda-lime glass. A thorough manufacturer search may surface only 5–10 qualified manufacturers globally who can produce Rise Jars at the required quality level. With fewer options, the founder has less leverage in price negotiations and fewer alternatives if a selected manufacturer fails to perform. Atlas should flag this as a supply chain risk and recommend the founder develop a backup manufacturer relationship even after selecting a primary partner.

**IP Risk from Manufacturer (High)**
Chinese glass manufacturers frequently serve multiple clients in overlapping product categories. The risk that a Rise Jars manufacturer produces a nearly identical product for a competing brand — using the knowledge gained from the Rise Jars production relationship — is real. The NDA provides contractual protection but enforcement against a Chinese manufacturer is difficult and expensive. The mitigation is: file a patent application before sharing designs, document the tooling ownership in the contract explicitly, and consider whether the most proprietary design elements (the exact stacking geometry, for example) are described in enough detail in public documents to establish prior art.

**MOQ Working Capital Requirement (High)**
At a 5,000-unit MOQ per SKU across five sizes, Rise Jars' first production run ties up 25,000 units of glass inventory. At a landed cost of $8–$15 per unit, that is $200,000–$375,000 in inventory before a single unit is sold. This is a significant working capital requirement that most bootstrapped founders cannot fund from personal resources. Atlas should flag this as a Stage 13 funding planning requirement and model the minimum production volume that makes the unit economics work at the lowest possible working capital exposure.

**Lead Time Mismatch with Launch Plan (High)**
If the founder plans to launch 8 months from now but the manufacturing lead time (tooling fabrication + production + freight) is 24–30 weeks (6–7.5 months), there is almost no margin for any delay. A single tooling revision, a production quality issue requiring a re-run, or a logistics delay can push the launch date by weeks to months. Atlas should ensure the manufacturing timeline is incorporated into Stage 14 launch planning with realistic buffer, and that the founder understands there is essentially no slack in a 8-month launch timeline with international glass manufacturing.

**Quality Control for Glass in Transit (Medium)**
Glass products break in transit. Even with proper packaging (foam inserts, double-walled boxes), a 1–3% breakage rate in ocean freight is not unusual for glass jars. The unit economics model must include a breakage allowance. The manufacturing agreement should include provisions for manufacturer replacement of units damaged due to inadequate packing (as opposed to carrier handling damage). Atlas should include transit breakage modeling in the unit economics model and flag the need for adequate packaging specification in the RFQ.

**Retail Channel Entry Requirements (Medium)**
If Rise Jars' launch strategy includes specialty retail (Williams-Sonoma, Sur La Table), those retailers require specific compliance documentation: FDA food contact compliance letters for all materials, California Prop 65 compliance confirmation, UPC barcodes and GS1 registration, specific packaging dimensions, and EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) capability for purchase orders. These requirements cannot be met by a new brand on day one without advance preparation. Atlas should flag these requirements at Stage 7 so the founder begins preparation in parallel with manufacturing, not after launch.

## Opportunities to Improve Atlas

**Gap: No Glass/Ceramics Manufacturing Guidance Module**
The Stage 7 blueprint guides the inventor through the manufacturing decision in a product-category-agnostic way. It describes the domestic vs. overseas trade-off, CM vs. OEM vs. ODM, and the RFQ construction — all correctly. **Gap: No Multi-Component Supply Chain Model**
Rise Jars is a multi-component product: glass body, lid, gasket, packaging. The Stage 7 blueprint assumes a single manufacturing source. Tooling investment is one of the largest capital expenditures in Stage 7 for a physical CPG product. Stage 7 does not surface the compliance requirements for specialty retail channel entry. These requirements — FDA food contact letters, Prop 65 compliance, GS1 barcodes, packaging dimensions, EDI capability — are long-lead items that take months to prepare. The BOM is the foundation of the unit economics model, the RFQ, and the manufacturing plan. Manually building a BOM from a design specification is tedious, error-prone, and typically results in missing components. Atlas should generate it automatically.

### 12. Tooling Cost Calculator

**What the founder wants:** A calculator that estimates the total tooling investment for a glass jar product line based on the number of unique molds, the manufacturing geography (China vs. Europe), and the part complexity. Shows how tooling cost amortizes per unit at different production volumes.

**Why it matters for Rise Jars specifically:** Tooling cost is the largest single capital requirement in Stage 7 for a glass product. The founder needs to know this number before committing to the product line architecture — not after. A five-size line with one universal lid costs more than twice as much to tool as a two-size launch. The tooling cost calculator makes this trade-off visible during Stage 5, when the line architecture is still being decided.

### 13. MOQ Impact Modeler

**What the founder wants:** A modeling tool that shows how minimum order quantity affects: (1) unit cost (higher MOQ → lower unit cost from volume pricing), (2) working capital requirement (higher MOQ → more capital tied up in inventory), and (3) break-even volume (higher MOQ → earlier break-even if sales velocity is sufficient, but higher risk if demand is softer than expected). Shows the optimal MOQ range given the founder's capital constraints and demand assumptions.

**Why it matters for Rise Jars specifically:** The MOQ for Rise Jars' glass jars (typically 5,000–10,000 units per SKU minimum at Chinese borosilicate glass factories) creates a significant working capital requirement. The MOQ impact modeler helps the founder understand whether she should negotiate a lower MOQ (higher unit cost, lower working capital risk) or commit to a higher MOQ (lower unit cost, higher working capital requirement). This is a business-critical decision that cannot be made intuitively.

**Rank: Must Have**

Exportable as a financial model for investor presentations.

**Why it matters for Rise Jars specifically:** Rise Jars' unit economics must work across three channels (DTC, specialty retail, Amazon) with different margin structures. # Part B1 Summary

## What Atlas Does Well in Stages 5–7

**Structured Three-Layer Design Framework**
Stage 5's organization of design work into Functional, Physical, and User Experience layers is genuinely valuable for a physical product founder. It prevents the most common design failure: building a product that functions correctly but fails to deliver a complete user experience. For Rise Jars, where the user experience (assembly, sealing, stacking, aesthetic delight) is as important as the functional specification, this framework is well-conceived.

**Manufacturing Implication Surfacing**
The Stage 5 blueprint's directive to surface manufacturing implications of design decisions in real time is one of Atlas's most valuable guidance commitments for hardware founders. Connecting "choosing injection-molded ABS" to "tooling costs of $3,000–$8,000 and MOQ of 1,000 units" in the moment of decision is far more valuable than discovering those implications during Stage 7 manufacturer engagement. For Rise Jars, this connection (choosing five glass jar sizes → tooling investment of $50,000–$150,000) is exactly the kind of insight that could change the line architecture decision.

**Prototype Testing Discipline**
Stage 6's emphasis on designing test protocols before building the prototype — defining pass/fail criteria for every Open Design Question — is sound engineering practice that most first-time founders skip. Without pass/fail criteria defined in advance, prototype testing degenerates into "it seems fine." Atlas's insistence on structured test protocols is the right call.

**Readiness States Over Numeric Scores**
The three-state readiness evaluation system (Not Ready / Getting There / Ready to Move Forward) is consistently the right approach for qualitative progress assessment. As validated in Part A, numeric scores create false precision. The three-state system allows Atlas to convey partial progress honestly without implying that 68% completion on a scale means something specific.

**Handoff Documentation**
The explicit handoff documentation at the end of each stage — naming exactly what information moves to the next stage and why — prevents context loss across a multi-stage process. For Rise Jars, the handoff from Stage 5 to Stage 6 (carrying the Product Design Specification, the Design Decision Log, and the Open Design Questions as the prototype testing agenda) and the handoff from Stage 6 to Stage 7 (carrying the prototype-validated revised specification) are correctly structured.

---

## What Is Missing

**The fundamental gap across all three stages is this: Atlas was designed for a generic physical product, but Rise Jars is a specialist product.** Borosilicate glass manufacturing has specific technical requirements, a specific manufacturer pool, specific tooling economics, and specific prototyping constraints that the current Stage 5–7 framework does not address. A founder who follows Atlas's current Stage 5–7 guidance exactly will reach Stage 7 with an incomplete specification, an underestimated tooling budget, no identified borosilicate glass manufacturers, and no framework for the three-phase prototyping process that glass products require.

The specific highest-priority gaps are:

1. **No glass product design workflow.** Stage 5 needs a materials-specific module for glass, ceramics, and other non-plastic rigid materials that addresses dimensional tolerances, formation processes, annealing requirements, and food contact compliance.

2. **No glass prototyping path.** Stage 6 needs an explicit three-phase prototyping path for glass products (form mock-up → proxy mechanism test → glass manufacturer sample run) with realistic cost and timeline ranges for each phase.

3. **No tooling economics module.** Stages 5 and 7 need a tooling cost calculator that makes the front-loaded tooling investment visible during design specification work — not after the manufacturer is selected.

4. **No NDA generator.** Stage 6 and Stage 7 both require NDAs before sharing designs with prototypers and manufacturers. Atlas identifies this requirement but does not fulfill it.

5. **No multi-component supply chain model.** Stage 7 assumes a single manufacturer. Rise Jars has at minimum three component suppliers. A multi-component unit economics builder is a Must Have for any physical CPG product with separate component sourcing.

6. **No borosilicate glass manufacturer discovery guidance.** The Stage 7 Future Integrations include generic manufacturing databases (Thomasnet, Maker's Row, Alibaba). None of these are likely to surface qualified borosilicate glass jar manufacturers. Atlas needs glass-specific supplier discovery guidance.

---

## Highest-Priority Improvements for Physical Product Founders

These are the improvements that, if implemented, would most significantly improve Atlas's value for a founder building a physical CPG product:

1. **Glass/ceramics/rigid materials product design module** — embedded in Stage 5 as a materials-type-specific guidance branch. Addresses tolerance profile, formation process, annealing, food contact compliance, and tooling economics. *Rank: Critical.*

2. **RFQ auto-generation from Product Design Specification** — Atlas should generate a manufacturer-ready RFQ document directly from the Stage 5 specification. This single feature eliminates hours of manual work and ensures the RFQ is complete and professional. *Rank: Critical.*

3. **Tooling cost calculator** — generates a tooling investment estimate based on the number of unique molds and manufacturing geography. Makes the front-loaded tooling commitment visible during design work, not after. *Rank: Critical.*

4. **NDA document generation** — generates an NDA appropriate for sharing designs with manufacturers and prototypers. Eliminates a compliance gap that Atlas correctly identifies but does not resolve. *Rank: Must Have.*

5. **Three-phase glass prototyping guidance** — explicit in-platform guidance on the 3D-printed form mock-up → machined proxy mechanism prototype → glass manufacturer sample run sequence, with cost ranges, timeline estimates, and supplier guidance for each phase. *Rank: Must Have.*

6. **MOQ impact modeler** — shows how MOQ decisions affect working capital requirements and unit cost, making the MOQ negotiation with manufacturers a data-driven decision rather than a guessing game. *Rank: Must Have.*

7. **Multi-component unit economics builder** — extends the Stage 7 unit economics model to aggregate costs from multiple component suppliers, with component-level cost tracking and landed cost calculation. *Rank: Must Have.*

8. **Manufacturing timeline builder** — generates a realistic timeline from design finalization to delivered inventory, accounting for tooling fabrication, production, sampling, freight, and customs. Connects directly to Stage 14 launch planning to prevent launch date setting without supply chain modeling. *Rank: Must Have.*
